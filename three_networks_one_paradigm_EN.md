# Three Networks, One Paradigm: Anatomy of the International Biopsychosocial ME/CFS Alliance

**Analytical Report — English Version**
*April 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The Oslo Chronic Fatigue Network (OCFN), Oslo Chronic Fatigue Consortium (OCFC), and COFFI (Collaborative on Fatigue Following Infection) represent an interconnected international infrastructure promoting the biopsychosocial model of ME/CFS — at the very moment that model faces its most serious scientific and institutional challenges. These three structures share overlapping personnel, coordinate messaging, and collectively work to preserve treatment approaches that the UK's NICE guidelines reversed in 2021. Their centre of gravity is Norway, where they influence guideline development, control major research funding, and shape public discourse — despite mounting biomedical evidence that ME/CFS involves immune, neurological, and vascular pathology fundamentally incompatible with psychological treatment frameworks.

This report maps the governance, personnel, funding, publications, and controversies surrounding these three networks, drawing on peer-reviewed literature, organizational websites, investigative journalism, patient community sources, and official Norwegian government documents. It covers the period from the PACE trial's 2011 publication through April 2026. Where claims are contested or sourced from advocacy materials, this is explicitly noted.

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM** is used throughout to flag factual assertions disputed between the parties.

## Part I: The PACE trial — origin story for a contested paradigm

### The £5 million trial that became a "medical scandal"

The PACE trial (*Pacing, graded Activity, and Cognitive behaviour therapy; a randomised Evaluation*) is the foundational study underpinning the biopsychosocial approach to ME/CFS and the direct intellectual ancestor of OCFN, OCFC, and COFFI. Published in *The Lancet* in February 2011, it enrolled **641 patients** diagnosed with CFS using the broad Oxford criteria and compared four arms: specialist medical care (SMC) alone, SMC + CBT, SMC + graded exercise therapy (GET), and SMC + adaptive pacing therapy (APT).

The three principal investigators — **Peter White** (Queen Mary University of London), **Trudie Chalder** (King's College London), and **Michael Sharpe** (University of Oxford) — reported that CBT and GET were "moderately effective," with recovery rates exceeding 20%. The trial cost £5 million, funded by the UK Medical Research Council, National Institute for Health Research, and the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP) — the first medical research the DWP ever funded.

*Sources: White et al., Lancet 2011; Tuller, Virology Blog 2015–2026; Wikipedia PACE trial.*

### Methodological criticisms that undermined the findings

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM** (disputed by PACE authors): The PACE trial's credibility collapsed under sustained scientific scrutiny. The most devastating criticism centres on **overlapping entry and recovery criteria**: participants needed to score below 65 on the SF-36 physical function scale to enter, but the recovery threshold was lowered mid-trial — from above 85 to above 60. Columbia University biostatistician **Bruce Levin** used PACE as a case study in a lecture titled "How NOT to Conduct a Randomized Clinical Trial."

The trial was unblinded, relying on subjective self-report outcomes. Objective measures — actometer-measured physical activity, fitness step-test, employment status — **showed no significant improvement** from CBT or GET, but these null findings were downplayed.

When Australian ME/CFS patient **Alem Matthees** filed Freedom of Information requests for anonymized data, Queen Mary University of London spent **£245,745** in legal fees fighting disclosure. The UK Information Tribunal ruled 2:1 in August 2016 to release the data. The subsequent reanalysis by **Wilshire et al. (2018, BMC Psychology)** found recovery rates dropped to **4–7%** across groups with no statistically significant differences — compared to the 22% originally reported. UK MP Carol Monaghan described PACE in a 2018 Parliamentary debate as "one of the greatest medical scandals of the 21st century."

*Sources: Wilshire et al. 2018; ME Association UK; UK Parliament.*

### David Tuller's decade of investigative journalism

**David Tuller, DrPH**, academic coordinator at UC Berkeley's School of Public Health, published his initial 15,000-word investigation "Trial by Error" on Virology Blog in October 2015. Now spanning over a decade, the series has documented protocol changes, insurance industry connections, and the Science Media Centre's role in shaping coverage. Tuller organized open letters signed by **over 100 experts** calling on *The Lancet* for an independent investigation. No PACE papers have been retracted.

In March 2026, Tuller published two articles directly concerning the Norwegian network: "Why is Professor Crawley Still on the COFFI Steering Committee?" and "More on Norway's Problematic Draft Guideline for 'Long-Term Fatigue — including ME/CFS'."

*Source: virology.ws/trial-by-error.*

### The NICE reversal and its aftershocks

On **29 October 2021**, NICE published guideline NG206, completely reversing its previous recommendations. GET was explicitly removed. CBT was reframed as coping support only. Post-exertional malaise (PEM) was recognized as a core diagnostic requirement. Three clinicians aligned with the CBT/GET approach resigned from the guideline committee before publication. The US CDC had already quietly dropped CBT and GET from its ME/CFS recommendations in **July 2017**. A January 2025 NICE surveillance review confirmed no new evidence warranting changes to NG206.

*Source: NICE NG206; NICE surveillance review January 2025.*

### Insurance industry connections

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM**: All three PACE principal investigators disclosed consulting relationships with insurance companies. Peter White served as chief medical officer for **Swiss Re**, where he presented PACE findings with implications for claims management. Michael Sharpe did paid consultancy for insurance and legal companies. The DWP's former Chief Medical Officer **Mansel Aylward** helped secure PACE funding; he subsequently became director of the Unum Provident Centre at Cardiff University. These relationships were disclosed in *The Lancet* but **not to trial participants** — which 42 scientists called "a serious ethical breach" violating the Declaration of Helsinki. PACE authors dispute that insurance connections influenced the trial.

*Sources: Virology Blog 2017; Socialist Health Association 2015.*

## Part II: Three organizations, one architecture

### OCFN — the institutional platform

The Oslo Chronic Fatigue Network describes itself as "an international network of **140 medical doctors, clinical psychologists, and researchers from 16 countries**" united by "a biopsychosocial understanding of fatigue-related conditions and pathways to recovery." It is hosted through **Silje Endresen Reme's** MindBodyLab at the University of Oslo's Department of Psychology and formally affiliated with the **European Association of Psychosomatic Medicine (EAPM)**.

The **six-person steering group** consists of:

| Member | Institution | Notable roles |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| **Silje Endresen Reme** (leader) | University of Oslo | OCFC corresponding author, MBRT developer |
| **Helena Liira** | Helsinki University Hospital | Former Editor-in-Chief, *Scand J Primary Health Care* |
| **Michael Sharpe** | Oxford (Emeritus) | PACE co-PI, EAPM President |
| **Trudie Chalder** | King's College London | PACE co-PI |
| **Paul Garner** | Liverpool (Emeritus) | COFFI CAC Chair, ex-patient |
| **Charlotte Ulrikka Rask** | Aarhus University Hospital | Child/adolescent psychiatry |

Helena Liira's dual role as former editor of the journal that published the OCFC's key paper is a significant declared conflict of interest. The OCFN website does not identify specific funding sources.

The network's media section contains an article on the nocebo effect arguing that media coverage of ME/CFS as a serious chronic illness can itself **create or amplify symptoms** in readers — explicitly directed at journalists with encouragement to tone down the severity of their coverage. Patient organizations view this as a narrative control strategy.

*Source: oslonetwork.no (all subpages retrieved April 2025).*

### OCFC — the manifesto vehicle

The Oslo Chronic Fatigue Consortium is not a permanent institution but rather a **group identity under which approximately 50 signatories published two controversial "manifest" papers in 2023**. The OCFN provides its organizational infrastructure.

**Paper 1: "Chronic fatigue syndromes: real illnesses that people can recover from"** (*Scandinavian Journal of Primary Health Care*, 2023) — Corresponding author Silje Endresen Reme. The 51 signatories argue symptoms "are more likely to reflect the brain's response to a range of biological, psychological, and social factors, rather than a specific disease process." Declared conflicts include: Trudie Chalder (royalties, insurance work); Hans Knoop (CBT manual royalties); **Live Landmark** (Lightning Process instructor payments); Helena Liira (former journal editor).

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM**: The article was published in the journal Liira herself recently edited. This is not illegal, but represents a role collision that would normally require explicit conflict-of-interest assessment.

**Paper 2: "Anomalies in the review process and interpretation of the evidence in the NICE guideline"** (*JNNP*, July 2023) — Lead author Peter White, with **55+ co-authors**. Two co-authors — Alan Carson (paid associate editor) and Jon Stone (editorial board) — held editorial roles at *JNNP*. NICE formally rebutted the paper in June 2024, stating the criticisms were "based on a misreading or misunderstanding of the guideline process."

*Sources: Tandfonline/SJPHC 2023; PubMed JNNP 2023; Virology Blog 2023.*

### COFFI — the research data pipeline

Initiated in **June 2015** and formally organized in **September 2020**, COFFI is hosted at **Akershus University Hospital (Ahus)** in Norway. It pools data from 15 cohort studies across more than 16 countries, with a total sample size of **24,405 participants** (as of May 2023). Ahus serves as formal data controller under GDPR.

**Governance structure:**
- **Steering Committee** (chaired by **Vegard Bruun Wyller**): Includes PIs of included cohorts including Andrew Lloyd, Hans Knoop, Rona Moss-Morris, and Esther Crawley
- **Scientific Advisory Board**: Includes Michael Sharpe, Simon Wessely, and Trudie Chalder
- **Consumer Advisory Committee** (chaired by **Paul Garner**): Includes **Tore Kristiansen** (chair, Recovery Norge) among others
- **Project Working Groups**: Must submit concept sheets to the Steering Committee for approval before accessing pooled data — giving the PI network effective gatekeeping control

The COFFI Consortium Agreement (revised 16 November 2023) reveals that **Peter White** was COFFI's founder and first leader; Wyller was appointed to succeed him. The Executive Committee has explicit mandate for "communication with public stakeholders, such as politicians, policy-makers, journalists and patients' organizations."

*Sources: coffi-collaborative.com (all subpages); COFFI Consortium Agreement PDF; UiO COFFI Health Registry.*

### How the three structures interconnect

The architecture functions as a **three-layer system**: OCFC provides the ideological framework (the two 2023 manifesto papers); OCFN provides the institutional platform and international network (140 members, steering group, EAPM affiliation); COFFI provides the research data pipeline (pooled cohort data, publication approval mechanisms). Recovery Norge serves as the designated patient voice through COFFI's Consumer Advisory Committee. Personnel overlap is extensive — every OCFN steering group member is an OCFC manifesto signatory, and key individuals hold roles across all three structures.

## Part III: The key individuals and their networks

### Vegard Bruun Bratholm Wyller — Norway's central node

**Professor at the University of Oslo and senior consultant at Ahus**, Wyller chairs COFFI's Steering Committee and signed the OCFC manifesto. His "sustained arousal hypothesis" (2009) proposes that CFS results from a permanent stress response perpetuated by illness beliefs and maladaptive cognitive processes. His own research has produced contradictory results: a clonidine trial testing his hypothesis **failed**, making patients worse. A 2026 hair cortisol study from his group indicated that fatigue may cause arousal rather than the reverse — the opposite of his theory.

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM**: In **February 2026**, Wyller secured **25 million NOK** via KLINBEFORSK for REHAB-FATIGUE, a multi-centre trial of Mind-Body Reprocessing Therapy (MBRT). Combined with the earlier 12 million NOK MINIRICO grant and 2.5 million NOK to his postdoc Maria Pedersen, his research group has received approximately **39.5 million NOK** in recent public funding. ME-foreningen and ME-foreldrene sent a joint concern letter to Helse Sør-Øst on 3 March 2026 regarding "disease understanding, patient safety and user involvement." Wyller uses Recovery Norge as patient representatives in his research — a practice questioned in *Tidsskriftet* as non-representative of the broader patient population.

*Sources: KLINBEFORSK allocations; lifewithmebysissel.wordpress.com; Tidsskriftet.no 2025.*

### Silje Endresen Reme — the academic architect

**Professor of Health Psychology at the University of Oslo**, Reme heads MindBodyLab, leads OCFN, and served as corresponding author on the OCFC manifesto. She holds an honorary researcher position at King's College London and developed MBRT, the intervention tested in Wyller's studies. Her lab published a pilot study on Lightning Process for adolescent cancer survivors (SSRN, 2021).

Freedom-of-information disclosures revealed that Reme and the Oslo Consortium were invited to provide expert input to the Helsedirektoratet guideline revision in December 2024, with meetings in February 2025. Biomedical researchers were not similarly engaged.

*Sources: UiO MindBodyLab; Science for ME forum; SSRN 2021.*

### The British PACE nexus transplanted to Norwegian soil

**Michael Sharpe**, **Trudie Chalder**, and **Simon Wessely** — all core figures in the original biopsychosocial model and PACE trial — hold formal positions in COFFI's Scientific Advisory Board and OCFN's steering group. Chalder was criticized in 2025–2026 for a paper redefining "recovery" to mean personal acceptance of illness. Sharpe receives royalties from CFS books and has done paid consultancy for insurance companies. Wessely co-developed the cognitive behavioural model with Chalder in 1989.

*Sources: Wikipedia PACE trial; MEpedia; Virology Blog 2026.*

### Paul Garner — from patient to gatekeeper

**Emeritus Professor at Liverpool School of Tropical Medicine**, Garner contracted COVID-19 in March 2020 and developed Long COVID. After initial BMJ blog posts validating patient experiences, he reported recovery through psychological/mind-body techniques and dramatically shifted positions. He now chairs COFFI's Consumer Advisory Committee and sits on OCFN's steering group — a dual role as "patient representative" and researcher promoting psychological approaches that critics view as fundamentally conflicted.

*Sources: MEpedia Paul Garner; BMJ blog series 2020–2021.*

### Esther Crawley and the SMILE study

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM**: Professor (now apparently retired) at the University of Bristol, Crawley led the SMILE trial testing Lightning Process for children aged 12–18 with CFS. The trial's **primary outcome was swapped** from school attendance (which showed null results) to self-reported physical function. A co-applicant was the research director of Phil Parker Ltd, the Lightning Process company. The paper required a 3,000-word correction; over 70 experts signed a letter to the *BMJ* editorial director. Crawley claimed death threats, but an FOI request found no incidents recorded by her university. She remains listed on COFFI's website despite apparent retirement; David Tuller noted this in March 2026.

*Sources: Virology Blog March 2026; BuzzFeed 2017; Science for ME.*

### Hans Knoop — CBT manual royalties and the Dutch connection

**Full Professor at Amsterdam UMC** and COFFI Steering Committee member, Knoop directs the Expert Centre for Chronic Fatigue and receives declared royalties from a published CBT treatment manual for ME/CFS. His ReCOVer trial of CBT for post-COVID fatigue showed null results on the objective actigraphy measure.

*Sources: Amsterdam UMC; Virology Blog.*

### Live Landmark — Lightning Process instructor turned PhD candidate

Former journalist reporting recovery from CFS via Lightning Process in 2007, **Landmark** has been an LP instructor since 2008 (courses: 21,000 NOK). She is a PhD candidate at NTNU supervised by Vegard Wyller, co-signed the OCFC manifesto as an "ex-patient," and co-founded Recovery Norge.

In **June 2021**, Norway's National Ethics Committee (NEM) **revoked ethical approval** for her LP study, citing untenable conflicts of interest as both researcher and commercial instructor. REC Mid Norway re-approved it in January 2022 with conditions. In 2011, a 13-year-old boy who had been advised to take an LP course — reportedly by Wyller — attempted suicide afterward.

**⚠ CONTESTED CLAIM**: The network disputes that the conflicts of interest affect research quality.

*Sources: ScienceNorway 2021 and 2022; MEpedia Live Landmark.*

## Part IV: Biomedical evidence contradicts the biopsychosocial framework

### DecodeME shatters the psychological narrative

The world's largest ME/CFS genetic study published initial results in August 2025 from the University of Edinburgh. Analyzing DNA from **15,579 cases** against 259,909 controls, it identified **eight genome-wide significant loci** near genes involved in **response to viral or bacterial infection**. Critically, the study found **no evidence** that ME/CFS genetic signals share causal variants with depression or anxiety — directly undermining the biopsychosocial premise. COFFI published a coordinated response warning against interpreting genetic findings as evidence of incurability.

*Sources: medRxiv preprint 2025; COFFI news September 2025.*

### Daratumumab at Haukeland shows 60% improvement

**Øystein Fluge and Olav Mella** at Haukeland University Hospital published a pilot study in *Frontiers in Medicine* (2025) testing daratumumab targeting long-lived plasma cells. Of **10 patients** with moderate-to-severe ME/CFS (Canadian Consensus Criteria), **6 experienced marked, durable improvement** — with mean SF-36 Physical Function scores rising from 25.9 to 55.0 (p=0.002). The larger **ResetME** randomized controlled trial (66 patients) opened recruitment in June 2025, funded partly by the Norwegian ME Association's 4 million NOK contribution.

Biomedical research funded by private ME patient donations contrasts sharply with the 39.5 million NOK in public funding flowing to Wyller's biopsychosocial programmes.

*Sources: PubMed 2025; Science for ME forum.*

### NIH deep phenotyping confirms biological basis

The NIH intramural study led by **Avindra Nath**, published in *Nature Communications* (February 2024), found brain abnormalities, **low catecholamines** in cerebrospinal fluid, immune dysregulation including "exhausted" adaptive immunity, and dramatic sex-based biological differences. Nath stated: **"The findings underscore that the symptoms cannot be explained by physical deconditioning or psychological factors."**

*Sources: NIH.gov press releases; Health Rising 2024.*

### Two-day CPET objectively demonstrates PEM

**Keller et al. (2024**, *Journal of Translational Medicine*)** compared 84 ME/CFS patients to 71 sedentary controls. ME/CFS patients showed reduced oxygen consumption and impaired energy production on day 2 **ruling out deconditioning** as an explanation and objectively demonstrating PEM — directly contradicting graded exercise therapy.

*Sources: Springer 2024; Bateman Horne Center.*

## Part V: The Norwegian battlefield

### Draft guidelines spark institutional crisis

On **4 February 2026**, Helsedirektoratet published a draft national guideline titled "Nasjonal faglig retningslinje for langvarig utmattelse, inkl. ME/CFS" for public consultation (deadline: 4 May 2026). Three of four patient organization representatives — from ME-foreningen, ME-foreldrene, and Covidforeningen — **formally dissented**. Only Recovery Norge did not dissent.

Freedom-of-information requests revealed that Reme and the Oslo Consortium were invited to provide expert input in December 2024, with meetings in February 2025. Biomedical researchers were not similarly engaged. Patient organizations representing over **8,000 members** issued a joint press release on 13 March 2026 demanding Norway follow international recommendations.

*Sources: helsedirektoratet.no; me-foreldrene.no; lifewithmebysissel.wordpress.com; Science for ME.*

### The Gulating court victory

In the landmark **Kolbjørn Kallevik case** at Gulating lagmannsrett (March 2026), a 24-year-old with severe ME since age 4 challenged NAV's denial of disability benefits. NAV called **Signe Flottorp** (FHI) as expert witness; she suggested the patient should "be a bit more positive." **The court overturned NAV's denial**, finding no available, relevant, or recommended treatment remained. The ruling establishes precedent that ME patients cannot be forced into treatments that may cause harm.

*Sources: Gulating lagmannsrett judgment 25.03.2026 (case no. 25-114554FØR-GULA/AVD1).*

### FHI and the October 2022 "secret seminar"

Key FHI personnel — including **Kjetil Brurberg**, **Signe Flottorp**, and **Per Magnus** — participated in an October 2022 seminar organized by Reme's MindBodyLab at FHI premises. Both Flottorp and Brurberg are OCFC manifesto signatories. Cochrane's December 2024 decision to abandon a promised new ME/CFS exercise review while republishing the contested Larun review with a 2024 date drew sharp international criticism.

*Sources: lifewithmebysissel.wordpress.com; The Sick Times 2025; ScienceNorway 2022.*

### KLINBEFORSK funding and Recovery Norge

Wyller's combined research funding of approximately **39.5 million NOK** for MBRT-based studies represents the largest investment in biopsychosocial ME/CFS research in Norwegian history. Critics describe MBRT as essentially rebranded Lightning Process. Recovery Norge, co-organized events with Wyller's research group; of 126 individual recovery testimonials on its website, **75 involve the Lightning Process**.

*Sources: KLINBEFORSK allocations; lifewithmebysissel.wordpress.com.*

## Part VI: Diagnostic criteria at the core of everything

The choice of diagnostic criteria determines which patients are studied, what treatments appear effective, and whether ME/CFS looks psychological or biological. **Oxford criteria** (1991, created by Michael Sharpe) require only chronic fatigue, with no PEM requirement. The **Canadian Consensus Criteria** (2003) mandate PEM as a core feature. COFFI uses Fukuda criteria. The US AHRQ recommended in 2016 that Oxford criteria be **retired** as they led to studies providing "no information about the effectiveness of the intervention" for actual ME/CFS patients. COFFI's 2023 research agenda paper referenced Sharpe's Oxford criteria — characterized by critics as attempting to resurrect abandoned definitions for Long COVID research.

*Sources: AHRQ 2016; IOM/NASEM 2015; Canadian Consensus Criteria 2003.*

### The institutional funding loop

In Norway, a pattern emerges: KLINBEFORSK funds Wyller's MBRT trials → studies generate evidence for biopsychosocial treatments → this evidence feeds Helsedirektoratet guideline processes where OCFC members provide expert input → guidelines recommend these treatments → NKT and clinical services implement them → Recovery Norge provides patient endorsement. Patient organizations describe this as a self-perpetuating system resistant to external evidence.

## Conclusion: A paradigm under siege, an infrastructure still standing

The three networks documented here — OCFN, OCFC, and COFFI — represent a sophisticated international infrastructure built to preserve and extend the biopsychosocial model of ME/CFS at a time when that model faces unprecedented challenges from every direction. The NICE reversal, the CDC's removal of CBT/GET recommendations, DecodeME's genetic findings showing no overlap with depression, the NIH's categorical statement that symptoms "cannot be explained by psychological factors," and the Haukeland daratumumab results all point toward ME/CFS as a biological disease requiring biological treatment.

Yet in Norway, this infrastructure retains substantial institutional power. Approximately **39.5 million NOK** flows to MBRT-based research. The guideline process was shaped by OCFC input while biomedical researchers were excluded. Recovery Norge, founded by LP practitioners, serves as patient representative in both COFFI and the guideline working group. FHI researchers who co-signed the OCFC manifesto serve as expert witnesses arguing ME patients should "be more positive."

The most significant finding of this research is not any single controversy but the **systemic architecture** connecting treatment development, evidence generation, patient representation, guideline influence, and funding allocation. Each element reinforces the others, creating what patient organizations describe as a closed loop resistant to external evidence.

The biomedical evidence accumulated between 2020 and 2026 — **8 genetic loci**, 60% daratumumab response, brain abnormalities documented by NIH, objective CPET evidence of exercise intolerance, 290+ blood biomarkers, and convergent findings from five countries on immune-vascular-metabolic pathology — now represents a fundamentally different understanding of ME/CFS from the one encoded in the PACE trial and promoted through these three networks. The Gulating court victory of March 2026 suggests cracks are appearing. Whether Norwegian health policy will respond rapidly enough to prevent further harm to the tens of thousands of patients living with a serious biological disease currently treated with methods disproven by the strongest scientific evidence ever assembled remains the central question.
